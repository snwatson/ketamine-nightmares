Image optimisation:

 

2D imaging:

Most important

·      Minimise demands on probe for best resolution

o   Goldilocks depth: object of interest mid-screen, not further away

o   Goldilocks sector width: important structures in view, not more

Approach

1.    Find the best interspace (i.e. heart springs into view)

2.    Find the best groove (i.e. image sharpens)

3.    Adjust the orientation (i.e. correct slice)

4.    Adjust the dials (i.e. best resolution - depth, width, gain)

Gating

·      End-diastole: peak of R wave (most things) or MV closure (for LVEF)

·      End-systole: end of T wave (most things) or smallest LV size (LVEF)

Cavities

·      No foreshortening: LV cavity should be long, LV apex should be still

·      Optimal gain: blood = black, tissue = white, endocardium = defined

Dimensions

·      For ventricles: follow the most continuous line, ignore the frilly bits, trace from annulus to annulus

·      For atria: ignore appendages and inflow veins

·      For aorta: leading edge to leading edge (google it)

·      For everything else: its intuitive

Valves

·      Pan through

·      Valves normal?

·      Annulus normal?

·      Open and shut?

 

Doppler imaging:

Most important

·      Ensure flow is in the direction of the beam (should be <20° off)

Colour doppler

(e.g. valve)

·      Increase sampling rate (Nyquist limit) to maximum

·      Reduce sampling rate slowly until just before speckling occurs

·      Pan through structure (e.g. valve for maximal regurgitation)

Spectral doppler

·      For LVOT: ensure the AV closing clip is in the waveform
